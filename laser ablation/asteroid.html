WHAT ARE ASTEROIDS ?

Asteroids are rocky, airless remnants left over from the early formation of our solar system about 4.6 billion years ago. Most of these objects are found in the asteroid belt between Mars and Jupiter, ranging in size from small pebbles to hundreds of kilometers in diameter. Unlike planets, asteroids do not have atmospheres or geological activity, making them relatively unchanged since their formation. They are composed mainly of rock, metal, or a combination of both, and some may contain valuable resources like water and rare metals. Though they usually remain in stable orbits, asteroids can occasionally stray close to Earth, becoming Near-Earth Objects (NEOs) and potential impact threats.

Asteroids Threats

Asteroids, small rocky bodies orbiting the Sun, pose a potential threat to Earth, though the risk of a significant impact is low. While small asteroids usually burn up in the atmosphere, larger ones could cause regional or global damage, with the most infamous example being the one that likely led to the dinosaurs' extinction. Agencies like NASA monitor Near-Earth Objects (NEOs) to assess potential risks, and strategies such as deflection or evacuation are considered for impact mitigation. Recent efforts, like NASA's DART mission, focus on demonstrating asteroid deflection technologies to protect Earth from future threats.

THE LASER ABLATION

Principles Of Laser Ablation

How Laser Ablation Works

Laser ablation involves the use of high-energy laser beams to vaporize and erode the surface of an asteroid. The intense heat and pressure generated by the laser interaction causes material to be ejected from the asteroid, resulting in a momentum change that can alter the object's trajectory.

Laser Wavelength and Power

The choice of laser wavelength and power output is crucial for effective asteroid ablation. Shorter wavelengths in the ultraviolet or visible spectrum are generally more efficient, as they can penetrate the asteroid's surface more effectively. Higher laser power also increases the momentum transfer, but must be balanced with practical considerations.

Beam Targeting and Focus

The laser-based approach allows for precise targeting and fine-tuning of the deflection, enabling more controlled and effective asteroid redirection.

Why To Use Laser Ablation?

Contactless Deflection

Laser ablation offers a contactless method of deflecting asteroids, avoiding the need for physical interaction or impact, which could be risky and unpredictable.
